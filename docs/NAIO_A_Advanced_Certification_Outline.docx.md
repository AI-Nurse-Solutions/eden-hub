# **1\. Program Identity & Position in the Pipeline**

NAIO-A (Advanced) is the second credential in the NAIO Institute’s stackable certification pathway. It bridges foundational AI fluency (NAIO-C) and the full Fellowship (NAIO-F) by converting knowledge into supervised operational authority. Where NAIO-C asks “Do you understand how to govern AI safely?” NAIO-A asks “Can you run it under pressure?”

## **Pipeline Position**

| On-Ramp | NAIO-C (Core) | NAIO-A (Advanced) | NAIO-F (Fellowship) |
| :---- | :---- | :---- | :---- |
| 10 Weekends to AI Fluency | Weeks 1–10 | **Weeks 11–20** | 6–9 month rotation |
| Awareness | Understand \+ Classify | Operate \+ Govern | Lead \+ Scale |
| No credential | Unit-level discipline | Multi-unit authority | System-level command |

# **2\. Eligibility & Prerequisites**

**Hard Requirement:** Active NAIO-C certification (passed within prior 12 months).

**Licensure:** Active RN license with a minimum of 3 years acute-care experience (ICU/ED/periop preferred).

**Recommended:** Current charge/supervisor role or equivalent operational authority. Sponsorship from unit manager or CNO encouraged.

**Application:** Submit NAIO-C capstone artifact, licensure verification, brief statement on intended AI governance use case in their unit/system. Max 500 words.

# **3\. Graduate Profile: Day-1 Capabilities After NAIO-A**

A NAIO-A graduate functions as a multi-unit clinical AI operator with supervised governance authority. This is not an AI hobbyist—this is the nurse who runs the go/pause/rollback discipline during an implementation.

### **Day-1 Capabilities**

* Run go/pause/rollback discipline for AI-enabled clinical workflows across assigned units using explicit thresholds and escalation paths

* Classify work by Green/Yellow/Red risk zones and enforce gating rules (what data, tools, and actions are permitted in each zone)

* Build, version, and propose Workflow Packs with required checks, gates, output templates, and prohibited actions

* Draft and present an AI Safety Case dossier covering risk profile, override logic, escalation pathways, and audit expectations

* Execute shadow-mode evaluations without clinical impact: measure edit distance, omission rates, citation coverage, near-misses

* Own the audit trail: what was asked → what was used → what was produced → what required human sign-off

* Build unit-level ROI narratives tied to throughput, staffing variance, and safety risk reduction (using NAIO ROI calculator)

* Lead near-miss reviews under just-culture approach with corrective actions and workflow pack updates

* Coordinate across authority boundaries: bedside command, attending orders, eICU surveillance, enterprise AI policy

### **What NAIO-A Does NOT Grant**

* System-wide delegated authority (requires NAIO-F)

* Autonomous deployment of new AI workflows without supervisor sign-off

* Board-level reporting authority (advisory role only at this stage)

# **5\. Curriculum Pillars (Weeks 11–20)**

Each pillar maps directly to the NAIO governance doctrine and the Five Iron Laws. The curriculum is 70% applied exercise, 30% structured content. Every week produces an auditable artifact.

## **Pillar 1: Clinical Systems Thinking & Operations Command**

*Weeks 11–12*

* Bottleneck analysis, queue theory applied to clinical units, throughput vs. utilization myths

* Interdisciplinary coordination: who owns what authority boundary in AI-enabled workflows

* Scenario: Model an ICU admission surge with AI-assisted triage—identify where the handoff fails

**Artifact:** Unit Operations Map with AI touchpoints, authority boundaries, and failure modes identified.

## **Pillar 2: Applied AI Fundamentals for Operators**

*Week 13*

* Automation vs. autonomy spectrum: what must never be autonomous in clinical settings

* Agent/orchestration architecture: what the NAIO operator needs to know (not build)

* Hallucination, drift, and failure modes: recognition patterns for clinical operators

* HIPAA/42 CFR Part 2 compliance boundaries for AI-generated content

**Artifact:** AI Failure Mode Recognition Checklist for their unit context.

## **Pillar 3: Workflow Pack Design & Risk Zoning**

*Weeks 14–15*

* Green/Yellow/Red zone classification methodology with clinical examples

* Workflow Pack standard: versioning, approval gates, prohibited actions, rollback triggers

* Build a complete Workflow Pack for a real use case from their unit (e.g., handoff draft, discharge summary, policy lookup)

* Peer review: cohort members red-team each other’s packs

**Artifact:** Production-ready Workflow Pack v1.0 with risk classification, gating rules, and rollback plan.

## **Pillar 4: Safety Case Dossier & Incident Management**

*Weeks 16–17*

* Safety Case methodology: risk profile, override logic, escalation pathways, audit expectations

* EDENA Safety Cards integration: ethical decision framework applied to AI governance

* Near-miss taxonomy and incident review under just-culture principles

* Tabletop simulations: “AI escalation failure,” “audit pipeline degraded,” “wrong-patient risk,” “model drift observed”

**Artifact:** Complete Safety Case Dossier for their Workflow Pack, plus one incident review write-up from simulation.

## **Pillar 5: Shadow Mode Operations & Evaluation**

*Week 18*

* Shadow mode discipline: running AI evaluation without clinical impact

* Metrics: edit distance, omission rates, citation coverage, hallucination detection, near-miss identification

* Verification-before-trust protocol: provenance, evidence, known vs. unknown gap documentation

* Data logging and audit trail management: what was asked → used → produced → signed off

**Artifact:** Shadow Mode Evaluation Report with quantified metrics from a simulated deployment.

## **Pillar 6: ROI Narratives & Stakeholder Communication**

*Week 19*

* Conservative ROI methodology: throughput gains, staffing variance reduction, OT/agency spend reduction

* Board-ready narrative construction: translating operational data into executive language

* Stakeholder mapping: CNO/CCO/CMO/CFO—what each cares about and how to frame AI governance value

* The “cost of not governing” argument: shadow AI risk quantification

**Artifact:** Unit-level ROI Brief (1-page) ready for presentation to their direct leadership.

## **Pillar 7: Governance Defense & Capstone**

*Week 20*

* Oral defense of complete governance portfolio to a simulated executive/governance panel

* Portfolio includes: Workflow Pack, Safety Case Dossier, Shadow Mode Report, ROI Brief, Incident Review

* Panel grades on: safety rigor, auditability, operational realism, communication clarity, ethical backbone

* Peer 360° evaluation: bedside credibility, IT partnership quality, leadership trust, “will they stop unsafe deployment?”

**Capstone Requirement:** Pass/fail defense with minimum 3.0/4.0 average across five competency domains (Clinical Credibility, AI/Systems Literacy, Governance/Risk, Operational Impact, Leadership). No domain below 2.0.

# **9\. Pathway Forward: NAIO-A to NAIO-F**

NAIO-A is not an endpoint. It is the proving ground that qualifies nurses for the full NAIO Fellowship—a 6–9 month supervised rotation with delegated system-level authority.

### **Fellowship Eligibility (after NAIO-A)**

* Active NAIO-A certification (passed within prior 18 months)

* 5–7+ years acute care with charge/supervisory credibility

* Demonstrated traits from NAIO-A: systems thinking, ethical backbone (“stop line”), executive communication under ambiguity

* Sponsor endorsement from unit/system leadership

* Portfolio review by NAIO Fellowship Admissions Panel

### **What Changes at Fellowship Level**

* Authority scope expands from multi-unit to system-wide

* 0.2–0.3 FTE secondment for 6–9 months producing auditable deliverables the system keeps using

* Board-level reporting authority (not just advisory)

* Delegated clinical authority for AI governance decisions

* Target outcome: Director of Clinical AI Operations / CNIO+ / Chief Clinical AI Officer track

# **10\. Ethics & Governance Commitments**

* Anti-retaliation protection: Fellows who pause or escalate unsafe workflows are explicitly protected under program policy

* No vendor-funded curriculum influence: any co-funding is governed with disclosed COI and firewall between funding and standards

* No credential lock-in: standards and controls are teachable and auditable; the Institute’s value is rigor \+ credentialing \+ conformance, not obscurity

* Nurse-led authority: the program is interprofessional by design but nurse-led because the credential grants (or prepares for) delegated clinical authority

* Zero Red Zone data exposures: all activities logged in compliance tracker; HIPAA/42 CFR Part 2 boundaries enforced throughout
